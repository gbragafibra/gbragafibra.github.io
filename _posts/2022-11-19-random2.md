# The cell and its decision making after a master Markov decision process


Consider a system at scale $\eta$, such that its decision making can be viewed as a Markov decision process (MDP) with discrete iterations over $\tau$. This MDP at scale $\eta$ can be thought as a master-MDP, given that at a smaller scale, for example $\eta - 1$, there are smaller components (themselves having decision processes as MDPs) comprising the system at scale $\eta$. Normally a MDP would have a state space $S$ (the set of states available to the system to transition into), an action space $A$, and the corresponding probability transitions between states (ex: $P_{i \rightarrow j}$) and their perceived rewards ($R_{i \rightarrow j}$) by the system after taking the corresponding action $a$. A further constituent of a MDP is the mapping function from $S \rightarrow A$, or a policy function $\pi$.


A question we might have is: Can we describe biological systems in general, after MDPs, and expanding this consideration over multiple scales? Given that a system, such as a cell, can be thought as a master-MDP, with constituents systems (at a smaller scale) - for example genetic circuits, metabolic circuits, and so on, being modeled after smaller-scale MDP's - would the policy functions $\pi$ of smaller-scale MDPs allign with the one given by the master-MDP, also taking the consideration of optimization of $\pi$. Afterall, if we have top-down control in these systems, wouldn't the previously stated consideration apply?
