# Fourier Transform and Heat Released by Cells

Lets converge to the description of a single cell with all its mechanisms over a period $\Delta \tau$, and the correspondent heat released $\Delta Q_{\tau_{i}}$ over each iteration $\tau_{i}$. If we appplied, in this case with a set of data points of $\Delta Q_{\tau_{i}}$, a discrete Fourier transform, we would get the decomposition to various sine waves with various frequencies $\omega$. Could we then ask the following question: Is each sine wave with frequency $\omega_{n}$, associated to a set point $\Delta_{n}$ the system (in this case the cell) oscillates around? Can we have a decomposition of all the synchronization mechanisms in the given cell with this approach? Would we trivially assume that sine waves with higher $\omega$ would be associated to processes (synchronized across the cell) with smaller temporal action, like simple enzymatic processes (without top-down control here, like major epigenetic regulation events)? Are control processes in a cell, even associated with heat released values that can be approximated by periodic functions?


---


Wouldn't it be simpler, to instead of keeping track of what would be happening overall in a cell (like a cell going into apoptosis) and associating major metabolites (and their functions) to it, to just recur to an encompassing metric like heat released? Can we keep going down this road, of solely trying to understand biological systems by characterization and classification of every event we see, without trying to unravel fundamental principles?
