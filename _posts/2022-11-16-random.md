# Perception and decision making mechanisms

If we have two primitive systems, $\chi_{1}$ and $\chi_{2}$, at competition with eachother, the heat released by transition is going to be given as tradeoffs between apparatus cost (and run cost), and the usefulnesss of not being totally optimal at predicting inputs from the environment (in this case, also implying that you are less predictable). Lets say that these two systems have masses, $m_{\chi_{1}} \simeq m_{\chi_{2}}$, such that according to $\Delta Q \propto m^{\alpha} \rightarrow \alpha_{\chi_{1}} \simeq \alpha_{\chi_{2}}$.

If for system $\chi_{1}$ we have the following input to output tables: $\Sigma^{in} = \{A\} \rightarrow \Sigma^{out} = \{B\}$, such that input $A$ can be attributed to an action taken by system $\chi_{2}$, we can clearly see that a deterministic decision making mechanism is present, resulting in the entropic cost for this decision being $S = 0\,J/K$. What is essentially not paid as an entropic cost for the decision taken, is paid either in energetic cost to mantain and run the apparatus making the detection and assuring the input to output table $\Sigma^{in} = \{A\} \rightarrow \Sigma^{out} = \{B\}$, or is paid evolutionarily as not being a viable strategy, given that system $\chi_{1}$ having a deterministic decision scheme, is more prone to be exploited by another system that would be able to detect $\Sigma^{in} = \{A\} \rightarrow \Sigma^{out} = \{B\}$ taken by $\chi_{1}$, and react accordingly. We also see that if two systems with deterministic decision making mechanisms are at competition, there will be convergence to an equilibrium in terms of competing strategies.

If on the other hand system $\chi_{1}$ has the following input to output table: 

$\Sigma^{in} = \{A\} \underbrace{\rightarrow}_{1/5} \Sigma^{out} = \{B\}$ 

and 

$\Sigma^{in} = \{A'\} \underbrace{\rightarrow}_{4/5} \Sigma^{out} = \{B'\}$

the entropic cost associated to the decision is going to be $S = -k_{B}((1/5)\ln(1/5) + (4/5)\ln(4/5)) \simeq 6.9\,\times\,10^{-24}\,J/K$. And supposedly, the energetic cost relative to the apparatus will decrease. Furthermore, given that this system, even if not completely optimal in terms of prediction, is less prone to be exploited, a certain character of stochasticity in terms of perception and decision making mechanisms, is a viable strategy evolutionarily, assuring a promotion for evolutionary differentiation.

What is curious is that for systems scaling up, with ever increasing values of $m$, the entropic cost per $kg$ of mass seems to decrease, almost telling us that these systems start becoming robust enough that a decrease in stochasticity (and associated entropic cost) in decision making is worth the trouble of possibly becoming more prone to exploitation by other systems. And in turn leading to the emergence of dynamics associated to top-down control, given that the number of associated states these systems have available for transition is relatively small (given their mass).
